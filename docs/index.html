Map Information

How to Use This Map

Zoom in to explore potentially noise-sensitive areas. Clicking features in the map:

  • Displays general information.
  • Updates the legend readout to noise levels at click.
  • Dynamically calculates noise distance decay estimates (roadways only).
  • Displays noise levels that meet or exceed EPA long-term guidance threshold of 55.0 dB.
  • Toggle layers on/off using the ☰ button.

Key Terms

dB
Decibels. This is the unit used to describe sound level. Higher dB values indicate louder sound.
AADT
Annual Average Daily Traffic. This is the average number of vehicles that travel along a roadway segment in a typical day over the course of a year.
BTS Mapped Value
The modeled transportation noise level shown by the Bureau of Transportation Statistics noise surface at the clicked location.
Distance Decay
The general reduction in sound level as distance from a source increases. In this map, simplified assumptions are used to estimate how roadway noise may decrease over distance.
Noise-Sensitive Features
Places where noise exposure may be especially important to consider, such as homes, schools, libraries, parks, and natural areas.
Area of Persistent Poverty (APP)
A Census Tract or county is classified as an APP if 20% or more of its population was living in poverty in the 1990 and 2000 decennial censuses, and in the 2022 Small Area Income and Poverty Estimates; or if a Census Tract has a poverty rate of 20% or more based on the 2014–2018 American Community Survey.

Overview

This map shows modeled transportation (railway, aviation, and highway) noise, traffic count information, screening-level distance-based exposure estimates, and environmental justice considerations. Noise-sensitive and noise-emitting sites are shown in the map.

Noise-emitting features include:

  • Roadways
  • Airports
  • Railway
  • Industrial Sites

Noise-sensitive features include:

  • Residential Homes/Areas
  • Schools
  • Libraries
  • Parks/Natural Areas

Transportation Noise Surface

Noise values shown on the map are based on the U.S. Department of Transportation Bureau of Transportation Statistics National Transportation Noise Map Data, downloaded for the state of Kentucky.

Traffic Counts

Roadway traffic is a critical environmental noise pollutant. Traffic data shown is derived from KYTC's ArcGIS REST Services TrafficCounts. Where available, AADT, single-truck (box truck, buses) AADT, and combo-truck (tractor-trailer) AADT are shown.

Environmental Justice

U.S. DOT Areas of Persistent Poverty (APP) are visualized by the percentage of BTS noise-exposed area (≥55.0 dB) within each APP Census Tract. In addition, the estimated number of noise-exposed residential units is summarized for each tract (when applicable) to highlight potential disparities in household-level impacts.

Estimated Noise Range

Noise ranges in road popups are estimated from the mapped noise value and simple distance-decay assumptions. Distance-decay assumptions are based on decay rates for hard ground (3.0 dB per doubling of distance) versus soft ground (4.5 dB per doubling of distance).

Note: Distance-decay estimates are simplified screening tools and may not align exactly with the modeled noise surface, which represents broader transportation noise exposure. These values are intended to help identify potentially noise-exposed areas, particularly for sensitive land uses. Actual noise conditions can vary based on local factors and should be verified through field measurements and detailed analysis.
